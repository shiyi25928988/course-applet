Question 1 (20分)

主题：市场失灵与政府干预 Market Failure

(a) Explain what is meant by a positive externality and use a diagram to show how it leads to market failure. [8]

(b) Evaluate three government policies that could be used to correct under-consumption of merit goods. [12]

📝 详细解答

(a)

Positive externality: benefit to third parties not directly involved in transaction.

MSB > MPB because of external benefits.

Example: education benefits individual and also society (more productive workforce, lower crime).

Market equilibrium occurs where MPB = MPC.

Social optimum occurs where MSB = MSC.

Since MSB > MPB, market quantity < social optimum quantity.

Under-consumption occurs - market fails.

Deadweight welfare loss triangle exists between market and social quantities.

(b)

1. Subsidies: Reduce price to consumers, increase consumption. Advantage: internalizes externality. Problem: government expenditure, elasticity of demand.

2. Direct provision: Government provides merit goods free at point of use (e.g., state education). Advantage: ensures universal access. Problem: opportunity cost, quality concerns, lack of choice.

3. Information provision: Educate consumers about benefits. Advantage: addresses root cause (imperfect info). Problem: slow effect, difficult to change behavior, government expenditure.

Evaluation should consider effectiveness, equity, efficiency, and implementation challenges for each policy.

Question 2 (20分)

主题：宏观经济目标 Macroeconomic Objectives

(a) Explain the possible causes of inflation. [8]

(b) Evaluate the view that the control of inflation should always be the main priority of government macroeconomic policy. [12]

📝 详细解答

(a)

Demand-pull causes: excess aggregate demand when economy near full capacity.

Caused by: low interest rates, high consumer confidence, fiscal expansion, currency depreciation.

Cost-push causes: rising costs of production passed to consumers.

Caused by: wage increases, raw material price rises (e.g., oil), imported inflation, higher taxes.

Monetarist view: inflation always monetary phenomenon - excessive money supply growth.

Expectations: high inflation expectations become self-fulfilling through wage-price spirals.

(b)

Arguments for priority:

Inflation creates uncertainty and discourages investment.

Reduced international competitiveness if inflation higher than trading partners.

Redistributive effects: lenders lose, borrowers gain.

Menu costs and shoe leather costs.

Hyperinflation can destroy monetary system.

Arguments against priority:

May conflict with other objectives (e.g., employment, growth).

Cost-push inflation may be temporary (supply shocks).

Low/moderate inflation may be necessary for growth.

Deflation may be greater risk in some situations.

Opportunity cost of policies used (e.g., high interest rates hurt investment).

Conclusion depends on context: inflation level, cause, country circumstances, and other economic conditions.
