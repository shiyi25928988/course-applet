Question 1 (16分)

主题：遗传 Genetics

(a) Explain the difference between codominance and incomplete dominance. [4]

(b) In humans, the ABO blood group system is controlled by three alleles: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, and i.

(i) Explain why this is an example of both multiple alleles and codominance. [3]

(ii) A man with blood group A and a woman with blood group B have a child with blood group O. Determine the genotypes of the parents and show the possible genotypes of their offspring. [5]

(c) Explain why males are more likely to express sex-linked recessive traits than females. [4]

📝 详细解答

(a)

Codominance: Both alleles expressed simultaneously in heterozygote (e.g., AB blood group where both A and B antigens present). Neither allele is dominant over the other - both are fully expressed.

Incomplete dominance: Heterozygote shows intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white → pink flowers). Neither allele is completely dominant. Blending effect occurs.

(b)(i)

Multiple alleles: More than two alleles exist for the gene (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i), although an individual only carries two.

Codominance: Iᴬ and Iᴮ are both dominant; when present together, both A and B antigens are expressed (blood group AB).

(b)(ii)

Child with group O must have genotype ii.

Therefore, each parent must contribute an i allele.

Father (group A): genotype Iᴬi

Mother (group B): genotype Iᴮi

Possible offspring genotypes: IᴬIᴮ (AB), Iᴬi (A), Iᴮi (B), ii (O) → 1:1:1:1 ratio

(c)

Sex-linked genes are located on the X chromosome.

Males have only one X chromosome (XY) while females have two (XX).

A recessive allele on X will always be expressed in males because no corresponding allele on Y to mask it.

Females need two copies (homozygous recessive) to express trait, but can be carriers (heterozygous) without expressing.

Question 2 (16分)

主题：基因技术 Gene Technology

(a) Describe the steps involved in producing recombinant DNA using plasmids. [6]

(b) Explain how genetic markers can be used to identify bacteria that have taken up recombinant plasmids. [4]

(c) Discuss the ethical considerations of gene therapy in humans. [6]

📝 详细解答

(a)

1. Isolate plasmid from bacterial cell (extrachromosomal circular DNA)

2. Cut plasmid with restriction endonuclease enzyme, creating sticky ends

3. Isolate desired gene from donor DNA using same restriction enzyme

4. Mix cut plasmid with donor gene - complementary sticky ends anneal by base pairing

5. Add DNA ligase enzyme to covalently join sugar-phosphate backbones

6. Result: recombinant plasmid containing inserted gene

(b)

Plasmids contain genetic marker genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance, GFP).

Original plasmid has two resistance genes: one for antibiotic A and one for antibiotic B.

Restriction site is within antibiotic B resistance gene.

If gene inserted successfully, antibiotic B resistance is disrupted.

Grow bacteria on both antibiotics:

- If grows on A only: took up recombinant plasmid (gene inserted successfully)

- If grows on A and B: took up non-recombinant plasmid (no gene inserted)

- If grows on neither: no plasmid taken up

(c)

Ethical considerations:

- Germ line therapy affects future generations vs somatic therapy affects only individual

- Risk of harmful side effects and immune responses

- Questions of informed consent for experimental treatments

- Potential for "designer babies" and genetic enhancement beyond therapy

- Access: only available to wealthy, increasing inequality

- Religious objections to manipulating human life

- Risk of creating new diseases through gene insertion

- Where to draw line between therapy and enhancement

Balance between potential benefits (curing genetic diseases) against risks and ethical concerns.
