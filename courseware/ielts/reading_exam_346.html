As you move, your brain gathers data from your environment-sights, sounds, smells, lighting, muscle contractions, a sense of time passingto determine which way your body has gone. The church spire, the sizzling sausages on that vendor’s grill, the open courtyard, and the train station-all represent snapshots of memorable junctures during your journey.   F. In addition to guidance and path integration, we use a third method for finding our way. An office worker you approach for help on a Manhattan street comer might say: “Walk straight down Fifth, turn left on 47th, turn right on Park, go through the walkway under the Helmsley Building, then cross the street to the MetLife Building into Grand Central.” This strategy, called route following, uses landmarks such as buildings and street names, plus directions straight, turn, go through—for reaching intermediate points. Route following is more precise than guidance or path integration, but if you forget the details and take a wrong turn, the only way to recover is to backtrack until you reach a familiar spot, because you do not know the general direction or have a reference landmark for your goal. The route following navigation strategy truly challenges the brain. We have to keep all the landmarks and intermediate directions in our head. It is the most detailed and therefore most reliable method, but it can be undone by routine memory lapses. With path integration, our cognitive memory is less burdened; it has to deal with only a few general instructions and the homing vector. Path integration works because it relies most fundamentally on our knowledge of our body’s general direction of movement, and we always have access to these inputs. Nevertheless, people often choose to give routefollowing directions, in part because saying “Go straight that way!” just does not work in our complex, man made surroundings. G. Road Map or Metaphor? 💡 解题技巧 Tips & Strategies

  1. Skimming（略读）：先快速浏览全文，掌握文章大意和结构，注意每段的首句和尾句
  2. Scanning（扫读）：根据题干中的关键词（人名、地名、数字等）回原文定位答案
